# PART A: THE ECONOMIC PROBLEM

---

## A.1 What is Economics?

**Economics** is the social science that studies how individuals, businesses, governments, and
nations make decisions about how to allocate **scarce resources** to satisfy **unlimited wants**.

Two core branches:
| Branch          | Focus                                             | Scale        |
|-----------------|---------------------------------------------------|--------------|
| **Microeconomics** | Individual consumers, firms, markets, prices   | Small-scale  |
| **Macroeconomics** | Whole economy — GDP, inflation, unemployment   | Economy-wide |

### Why Economics Exists — The Fundamental Problem
Resources are **scarce** (limited). Human wants are **unlimited**.
This mismatch creates the need to **choose** — and every choice has a cost.

---

## A.2 Scarcity

**Scarcity** is the condition that arises because human wants exceed the available resources.

### Key Distinction: Scarcity vs Shortage
| Term       | Meaning                                                          |
|------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Scarcity**  | A permanent condition — wants always exceed resources         |
| **Shortage**  | A temporary imbalance — supply < demand at the current price  |

Scarcity affects **everyone** — even the wealthiest individuals face a scarcity of time.

### What is Scarce?
- Natural resources (land, oil, water, minerals)
- Labour (there are only so many workers with particular skills)
- Capital (machines, factories, technology)
- Time (the most universally scarce resource)

### What is NOT Scarce? (Free Goods)
A **free good** is one available in unlimited supply at zero cost.
- Sunlight (generally)
- Air (in most places)

**Important:** Even these can become scarce — clean air in polluted cities, fresh water in droughts.

---

## A.3 Wants vs Needs

| Term     | Definition                                          | Examples                        |
|----------|-----------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Needs** | Essentials for survival                             | Food, shelter, clean water, health care |
| **Wants** | Desires beyond basic needs; unlimited              | Cars, smartphones, holidays, fashion |

Economists focus on **wants** because:
- Needs are relatively fixed and small in number
- Wants are unlimited — satisfying one creates more
- Most economic decisions are about wants, not survival

---

## A.4 Opportunity Cost — The Core Concept

**Opportunity cost** is the value of the **next best alternative forgone** when a choice is made.

> Opportunity cost is NOT simply "what you gave up" — it is specifically the **BEST alternative** you gave up.

### Formula Thinking (not literal formula):
```
Opportunity cost of choosing A = Value of the best alternative (not A) that was given up
```

### Examples

**Individual level:**
You have Saturday afternoon free. You can either:
- Work a 4-hour shift earning $80
- Study for an exam (no monetary value, but important)
- Watch TV

If you choose to watch TV, the opportunity cost is studying (assuming that was the next best use).
If you choose to work, the opportunity cost is studying (or watching TV — whichever you valued most).

**Business level:**
A factory can produce 1,000 units of Product A OR 2,000 units of Product B (not both).
If it produces Product A, the opportunity cost = the 2,000 units of Product B forfeited.

**Government level:**
The government has $500 million. It can build hospitals OR roads (not both).
If it builds hospitals, the opportunity cost = the roads not built.

### Opportunity Cost is Always Implicit
Even "free" things have opportunity costs — the opportunity cost of attending a free concert is
whatever you would have done with that time instead.

---

## A.5 The Three Fundamental Economic Questions

Every society must answer:

| Question               | Meaning                                           |
|------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| **WHAT** to produce?   | Which goods and services? How much of each?       |
| **HOW** to produce?    | Which resources and methods? Labour-intensive or capital-intensive? |
| **FOR WHOM** to produce? | Who gets the output? How is it distributed?   |

Different economic systems answer these questions differently (see Part C).

# PART B: FACTORS OF PRODUCTION

## B.1 The Four Factors

Resources used in production are called **factors of production** (or productive resources).

| Factor          | Definition                                      | Reward      | Examples                              |
|-----------------|-------------------------------------------------|-------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Land**        | All natural resources                           | Rent        | Soil, forests, minerals, water, oil   |
| **Labour**      | Human physical and mental effort                | Wages/Salary| Workers, managers, professionals      |
| **Capital**     | Man-made tools used in production               | Interest    | Machines, factories, computers, roads |
| **Enterprise**  | The ability to organise and take risk           | Profit      | Entrepreneurs, business founders      |

### Critical Distinctions

**Capital (economic) vs Capital (everyday speech):**
- Economic capital = physical tools of production (machines, equipment)
- Everyday "capital" often means money — but money is NOT a factor of production
- Money is used to BUY factors; it is not itself a productive resource

**Land (economic) vs Land (everyday):**
- Economic land = ALL natural resources, not just soil
- Includes: mineral deposits, fisheries, the electromagnetic spectrum, water bodies

**Human Capital:**
A related concept — skills, education, and training that increase labour productivity.
When a nurse completes a degree, their human capital rises.

# PART C: ECONOMIC SYSTEMS

---

## C.1 Overview of Economic Systems

An **economic system** is the way a society organises the production, distribution, and
consumption of goods and services — i.e., how it answers the three fundamental questions.

| System          | Who Decides?              | Property Ownership | Examples            |
|-----------------|---------------------------|--------------------|---------------------|
| **Traditional** | Custom, culture, habit    | Communal/tribal    | Indigenous communities |
| **Market**      | Prices/Supply & Demand    | Private            | USA (most), Hong Kong |
| **Planned/Command** | Central government   | State              | Soviet Union, North Korea |
| **Mixed**       | Both markets AND government | Mix of both     | Australia, UK, Canada |

---

## C.2 Traditional Economy

**How it works:** Decisions based on customs, traditions, and inherited practices.

**Characteristics:**
- Little change over time — production methods passed down generations
- Often based on subsistence (producing just enough to survive)
- Community-focused, not profit-focused

**Limitations:**
- Does not adapt to change
- Cannot harness modern technology efficiently
- Living standards remain low

---

## C.3 Market Economy (Free Enterprise / Capitalism)

**How it works:** Prices determined by supply and demand guide decisions. Private individuals
and firms own resources and decide what to produce.

### Key Features
- **Private property rights:** individuals own their resources and can use them freely
- **Price mechanism:** prices signal what to produce and how much
- **Profit motive:** firms produce what is profitable → drives efficiency
- **Competition:** multiple firms compete → better products, lower prices
- **Freedom of choice:** consumers choose what to buy; workers choose where to work

### The Invisible Hand (Adam Smith, 1776)
Adam Smith argued that individuals pursuing their own self-interest are "led by an invisible hand"
to promote the public good — they create goods people want, employ workers, and generate wealth
without any central direction.

### Advantages
- Efficient allocation of resources (market decides)
- Encourages innovation and entrepreneurship
- Wide variety of goods/services
- Individuals have freedom

### Disadvantages
- Market failures: public goods (defence, street lights) may be under-provided
- Negative externalities: pollution may be ignored
- Inequality: wealth concentrates — some get very rich, others remain poor
- No safety net: unemployment, illness, old age become personal problems

---

## C.4 Planned (Command) Economy

**How it works:** The government (central authority) makes all decisions about what to produce,
how to produce it, and how to distribute it.

### Examples
- Soviet Union (1917–1991)
- Cuba (partially)
- North Korea

### Advantages
- Can mobilise resources rapidly toward national goals (war, space race)
- Theoretically eliminates unemployment (government provides all jobs)
- More equal distribution of income
- Can focus on social goods (healthcare, education) regardless of profitability

### Disadvantages
- Lack of incentive: no profit motive → inefficiency, low quality
- Central planners cannot process millions of pieces of information that prices carry
- Shortages and surpluses common (prices not used to balance supply and demand)
- Suppression of individual freedom
- Historical record: poor economic performance vs market economies

---

## C.5 Mixed Economy — Australia's System

**How it works:** Combines private markets with government intervention. Most economies today
are mixed economies.

### Government's role in a mixed economy:
1. **Regulate** markets (competition law, environmental standards, safety regulations)
2. **Provide** public goods (defence, roads, courts)
3. **Redistribute** income (welfare, progressive taxation)
4. **Correct** market failures (pollution taxes, subsidies for education)
5. **Stabilise** the economy (fiscal and monetary policy)

### Australia's Mixed Economy
Australia is primarily a market economy with significant government involvement:
- Private sector drives most production (businesses)
- Government provides: Medicare, public schools, defence, roads, welfare
- RBA (Reserve Bank of Australia) manages monetary policy
- Government regulates: ACCC (competition), ASIC (financial markets), EPA (environment)

# PART D: THE PRODUCTION POSSIBILITY FRONTIER (PPF)

---

## D.1 What is the PPF?

The **Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)** — also called the Production Possibility Curve (PPC)
— shows the **maximum combinations** of two goods that an economy can produce using all available
resources **efficiently**.

Points:
- **ON the PPF:** production is efficient (all resources fully and best used)
- **INSIDE the PPF:** production is inefficient (resources wasted, unemployed, or misallocated)
- **OUTSIDE the PPF:** currently **unattainable** (beyond current capacity)

---

## D.2 Drawing and Reading a PPF

```
  Consumer
  Goods
  (units)
    |
  8 |●  A (efficient)
    |  \
  6 |   \  B (efficient)
    |    \
  4 |     \  C (efficient) ← on the frontier
    |      \
  2 |    X  \
    |  (inefficient)\ D (efficient)
  0 +────────────────●───────────
    0   2   4   6   8   10     Capital Goods (units)

Point A = (0, 8):   All resources to consumer goods
Point D = (10, 0):  All resources to capital goods
Points A, B, C, D:  ON the PPF — efficient
Point X (3, 3):     INSIDE — inefficient (wasted resources)
Any point outside:  Unattainable currently
```

---

## D.3 The Shape of the PPF — Concave (Bowed Out)

The PPF is typically drawn as a **concave curve** (bowed outward from the origin), NOT a straight
line. This reflects the **Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost**.

### Why? Specialisation of Resources

Resources are not equally suited to producing all goods. When you transfer the BEST-suited
resources first, production of the new good rises a lot. As you transfer more, you are forced
to use resources less suited to the new good — and you gain less and less.

```
Example: Shifting from capital goods to consumer goods

Transfer 1st workers (those best at making consumer goods):
  → Gain many consumer goods, lose few capital goods     (low OC)

Transfer 2nd workers (less suited to consumer goods):
  → Gain fewer consumer goods, lose more capital goods   (higher OC)

Transfer 3rd workers (very poorly suited):
  → Gain very few consumer goods, lose many capital goods (very high OC)
```

This is why the curve bows OUT — each additional unit of consumer goods requires giving up
MORE and more capital goods.

### The Straight-Line PPF (Constant Opportunity Cost)
A straight-line PPF would imply resources are **perfectly substitutable** — equally good at
producing both goods. This rarely happens in practice but is used for simplification.

---

## D.4 Opportunity Cost on the PPF

The opportunity cost of producing more of Good X = the amount of Good Y that must be given up.

```
Suppose:
  - Moving from point B (6 consumer, 4 capital)
  - to point C (4 consumer, 6 capital)

You gain 2 units of capital goods.
You lose 2 units of consumer goods.

Opportunity cost of 2 capital goods = 2 consumer goods
(or: opportunity cost of 1 capital good = 1 consumer good at this point)
```

**On a concave PPF, opportunity cost INCREASES as you produce more of one good.**

---

## D.5 Shifts of the PPF — Economic Growth and Contraction

The entire PPF can **shift outward** (growth) or **inward** (contraction).

### Outward Shift (Economic Growth / Increase in Productive Capacity)
Occurs when:
- **More resources:** population growth, discovery of new resources
- **Better technology:** same resources produce more output
- **Improved education/training:** increase in human capital (labour becomes more productive)
- **New capital:** investment in machinery and infrastructure

```
              Consumer
              Goods
                |        /  New PPF (outward shift)
                |      /  /
                |    /  /  ← economy can now produce combinations
                |  /  /      previously unattainable
                |/  /
                +──/─────────────── Capital Goods
```

### Inward Shift (Economic Decline / Reduction in Capacity)
Occurs when:
- Natural disaster destroys resources
- War damages capital
- Significant emigration reduces labour force
- Environmental degradation

### Biased (Asymmetric) Shift
If technology improves only for one sector, the PPF shifts outward on ONE axis only.

```
Example: Agricultural revolution only

Consumer   |      ←  PPF shifts outward on consumer axis only
Goods      |     /  (new PPF)
           |   /  /
           |  / /
           | //
           |/──────────────── Capital Goods

The y-axis endpoint moves up; x-axis endpoint stays fixed.
```

---

## D.6 PPF and Economic Concepts

| PPF Concept              | What it Illustrates               |
|--------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| On the frontier          | Productive efficiency             |
| Inside the frontier      | Inefficiency (unemployment, waste)|
| Outside the frontier     | Unattainable (current capacity)   |
| Movement along frontier  | Reallocation — opportunity cost   |
| Shift outward            | Economic growth                   |
| Concave shape            | Law of increasing opportunity cost|
| Straight-line PPF        | Constant opportunity cost         |

# PART E: RESOURCE ALLOCATION AND ECONOMIC EFFICIENCY

---

## E.1 What is Efficiency?

**Efficiency** means getting the maximum output from available resources — no waste.

### Three Types of Efficiency

| Type                      | Meaning                                              |
|---------------------------|------------------------------------------------------|
| **Productive efficiency** | Producing at maximum output with given resources (on the PPF) |
| **Allocative efficiency** | Producing the combination of goods that best satisfies society's wants |
| **Dynamic efficiency**    | Efficiently creating new technology and innovation over time |

For Year 11, focus on **productive efficiency** (on the PPF = efficient; inside = inefficient).

---

## E.2 The Role of Prices in Allocation

In a market economy, **prices** are the signal that tells:
- **Producers:** what to make (high price = profitable = produce more)
- **Consumers:** what to buy (high price = buy less, seek alternatives)
- **Resource owners:** where to send resources (high wages in an industry = move there)

### The Price Mechanism
```
Demand rises for good X
→ Price of X rises
→ Profit signal: more revenue for producers of X
→ More firms enter / existing firms produce more X
→ Supply of X rises
→ Price falls back toward equilibrium
→ Consumers get more of what they wanted
```

This automatic coordination is the "invisible hand" in action — no central planner required.

---

## E.3 Market Failure

Markets sometimes fail to allocate resources efficiently. This justifies government intervention.

### Causes of Market Failure

**1. Public Goods**
Goods that are **non-rival** (one person using it doesn't reduce availability for others) and
**non-excludable** (can't stop non-payers from using it).
Examples: national defence, street lights, public fireworks.

The **free rider problem:** people consume the good without paying → private firms won't provide it
→ government must provide and fund through taxation.

**2. Externalities**
Costs or benefits that fall on third parties not involved in the transaction.

| Type                    | Description                          | Example              |
|-------------------------|--------------------------------------|----------------------|
| Negative externality    | Third parties bear a cost            | Factory pollution    |
| Positive externality    | Third parties gain a benefit         | Your vaccination     |

Without intervention, markets over-produce goods with negative externalities and under-produce
goods with positive externalities.

**3. Information Failure**
When buyers or sellers lack necessary information to make good decisions.
Example: Used car market — buyer doesn't know the car's true condition.

**4. Market Power (Monopoly)**
When one firm dominates a market, it can raise prices above competitive levels, reducing output
and consumer welfare.

# PART F: THE CIRCULAR FLOW OF INCOME

---

## F.1 Simple Two-Sector Model

The most basic model of the economy has two sectors:
- **Households** — own resources, buy goods and services
- **Firms** — use resources, produce goods and services

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
                    │           PRODUCT MARKETS               │
                    │  (goods and services bought and sold)   │
                    └────────────┬──────────────┬─────────────┘
                                 │              │
              Households ←── Revenue ←── Goods/Services ←── Firms
              spend income       │              │             earn revenue
                                 │              │
              Households ──► Factors ──► Factor payments ──► Firms
              supply resources   │              │             use resources
                    ┌────────────┴──────────────┴─────────────┐
                    │           FACTOR MARKETS                 │
                    │  (land, labour, capital, enterprise)     │
                    └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Two flows:**
1. **Real flow:** resources flow from households to firms; goods/services flow back
2. **Money flow:** wages/rent/profit flow from firms to households; spending flows back

---

## F.2 Five-Sector Model (Full Model)

The five-sector model adds:
- **Financial sector** (banks — savings → investment)
- **Government** (taxes → government spending)
- **Overseas sector** (imports → exports)

### Injections and Leakages

| Leakages (withdrawals)         | Injections (additions)       |
|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Saving (S)                    | Investment (I)               |
| Taxation (T)                  | Government spending (G)      |
| Imports (M)                   | Exports (X)                  |

**Leakages** reduce the circular flow — money leaves the spending stream.
**Injections** increase the circular flow — money enters the spending stream.

### Equilibrium Condition
```
Economy in equilibrium when:
Total leakages = Total injections
S + T + M = I + G + X
```

If injections > leakages → economy grows (incomes rise, output expands)
If leakages > injections → economy contracts (incomes fall, output shrinks)

---

## F.3 National Income Measures

Three equivalent approaches to measuring total output (all give the same answer):

| Method               | What it Measures                                 |
|----------------------|--------------------------------------------------|
| **Expenditure (GDP)** | Total spending on final goods and services      |
| **Income**           | Total income earned by factors of production    |
| **Output/Production**| Total value added at each stage of production   |

**GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)**
- C = Household consumption expenditure
- I = Business investment expenditure
- G = Government expenditure
- X = Exports
- M = Imports

# PART G: ECONOMIC GOALS AND TRADE-OFFS

---

## G.1 Australia's Economic Goals

| Goal                           | Target/Indicator                          |
|--------------------------------|-------------------------------------------|
| **Strong economic growth**     | GDP growth ~3% per year                   |
| **Low unemployment**           | Unemployment rate ~4–5%                   |
| **Low inflation**              | CPI inflation 2–3% per year (RBA target)  |
| **External stability**         | Manageable current account deficit        |
| **Environmental sustainability** | Protecting natural resources            |
| **Equitable income distribution** | Reducing poverty and inequality        |

---

## G.2 The Trade-Off Between Goals

Economic goals often conflict — improving one can worsen another.

### Inflation vs Unemployment Trade-Off (Phillips Curve)
The classic trade-off: lower unemployment tends to push up inflation.

```
Inflation
    |
  5%|   ●  A
    |      \
  3%|       \  ●  B
    |          \
  1%|           \  ●  C
    |
    +──────────────────── Unemployment
       3%   5%   7%

Moving from C to A: unemployment falls, but inflation rises.
Moving from A to C: inflation falls, but unemployment rises.
```

**Why:** When unemployment is low, workers have bargaining power → wages rise → costs rise → inflation.
When unemployment is high, workers accept lower wages → cost pressures ease → inflation falls.

### Growth vs Environment Trade-Off
Rapid economic growth often depletes natural resources and increases pollution.
Choosing environmental protection may slow growth.

### Growth vs Equity Trade-Off
Policies that boost growth (lower taxes, less regulation) sometimes increase income inequality.
Policies that reduce inequality (higher taxes on rich, more welfare) may reduce growth incentives.

---

## G.3 Positive vs Normative Economics

This distinction is crucial in Year 11.

| Type          | Definition                                       | Example                                           |
|---------------|--------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| **Positive**  | Factual statements — can be tested and verified  | "Unemployment is 4.2%" or "Raising the minimum wage reduces jobs (empirically)" |
| **Normative** | Value judgements — involve opinions and values   | "The government SHOULD reduce inequality" or "Economic growth is more important than the environment" |

Positive statements are about **what IS** — objective, testable.
Normative statements are about **what OUGHT TO BE** — subjective, debatable.

**Tricky cases:**
- "Inflation causes unemployment" → Positive (can be studied empirically)
- "Inflation is worse than unemployment" → Normative (value judgement)
- "The minimum wage is $23.23/hour" → Positive (factual)
- "The minimum wage should be raised to $30/hour" → Normative (opinion)

# PART H: DEMAND AND SUPPLY — INTRODUCTION

---

## H.1 The Law of Demand

**As price rises, quantity demanded falls (all else equal — ceteris paribus).**

This reflects:
- **Substitution effect:** higher price makes alternatives look more attractive
- **Income effect:** higher price reduces real purchasing power → less can be bought

### The Demand Curve

```
  Price
    |
  $8|●
    |  \
  $6|   ●
    |    \
  $4|     ●
    |      \
  $2|       ●
    |        \
    +──────────────── Quantity Demanded
    0  10  20  30  40
```

Downward sloping: inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.

### Non-Price Determinants of Demand (Demand Shifters)

These shift the entire demand curve (not movement along it):

| Shifter                     | Effect                                             |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Income rises                | Demand rises for normal goods; falls for inferior goods |
| Price of substitute rises   | Demand rises (e.g., tea rises when coffee gets expensive) |
| Price of complement falls   | Demand rises (e.g., printers sell more when ink is cheap) |
| Tastes/preferences          | Greater preference → demand rises                  |
| Population grows            | More buyers → demand rises                         |
| Future price expected to rise | Buy now → demand rises today                    |

---

## H.2 The Law of Supply

**As price rises, quantity supplied rises (all else equal — ceteris paribus).**

Higher prices make production more profitable → firms supply more.

### The Supply Curve

```
  Price
    |         /●
  $8|       /●
    |     /●
  $4|   /●
    | /●
  $2●
    +──────────────── Quantity Supplied
    0  10  20  30  40
```

Upward sloping: positive relationship between price and quantity supplied.

### Non-Price Determinants of Supply (Supply Shifters)

| Shifter                     | Effect                                             |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Cost of production falls    | Supply rises (more profitable at every price)     |
| Technology improves         | Supply rises (cheaper/faster to produce)          |
| Number of producers rises   | Supply rises                                       |
| Input prices fall (wages, materials) | Supply rises                           |
| Government subsidy          | Supply rises                                       |
| Tax on production           | Supply falls                                       |
| Future price expected to rise | Withhold supply today → supply falls today      |

---

## H.3 Market Equilibrium

**Equilibrium** is the price at which quantity demanded = quantity supplied.

```
  Price
    |        S
    |      /
  $6|────/────────── equilibrium price
    |   ×           Qd = Qs = 25
    |  / \
    | /   \
    |/     D
    +──────────────── Quantity
         25
```

### Surplus and Shortage

**Surplus (excess supply):** Price is ABOVE equilibrium → Qs > Qd → price falls
**Shortage (excess demand):** Price is BELOW equilibrium → Qd > Qs → price rises

```
     $8 ─── ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─    ← surplus at $8 (supply > demand)
      |       \     /
     $6 ────── × ────────  ← equilibrium
      |      /   \
     $4  ── ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─    ← shortage at $4 (demand > supply)
```

---

## H.4 Market Changes — Worked Through

**Example: A drought hits Australia's wheat farmers.**

```
Effect on the wheat market:
- Drought destroys crops → supply of wheat FALLS
- Supply curve shifts LEFT

New equilibrium:
- Price RISES
- Quantity FALLS

Draw:
  Price
    |    S₂  S₁
    |   / |/
  $8|──×  /          ← new equilibrium (higher P, lower Q)
    | /  ×           ← old equilibrium
  $6|/  /
    |  /  D
    +──────────── Quantity
      Q₂ Q₁

P rises from $6 → $8
Q falls from Q₁ → Q₂
```

## Answers to HSC Questions

---

### Short Answer Answers

**1.** Opportunity cost: the value of the next best alternative forgone when a choice is made.
Example: A student who chooses to spend Saturday studying gives up the income they could
have earned by working — this forgone income is the opportunity cost.

**2.** Positive: objective, factual, testable. Normative: subjective, value-laden.
(a) "The unemployment rate is 4.5%" → **Positive** — this is a measurable fact.
(b) "The government should spend more on education" → **Normative** — "should" implies a value judgement.

**3.** The concave shape reflects the **Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost**. Resources are not
equally suited to producing all goods. When an economy initially shifts resources from Good A to
Good B, it moves the resources MOST suited to B — achieving large gains in B at little cost to A.
As more resources are transferred, increasingly less-suited resources are moved, so each additional
unit of B requires giving up more and more A. This rising opportunity cost produces the bowed-outward
shape.

**4.** Any TWO from:
- **Technological advance:** New technology allows the same quantity of resources to produce
  more output, shifting the PPF outward.
- **Population growth (labour increase):** More workers increase the economy's productive capacity.
- **Capital investment:** Building more factories, machines expands what can be produced.
- **Discovery of new natural resources:** Increases the availability of raw material inputs.
- **Improvement in human capital:** Education/training makes labour more productive.

**5.**
- **Leakage:** money that flows out of the circular income flow. Example: savings deposited in banks — this money is not immediately spent on goods and services.
- **Injection:** money that flows INTO the circular income flow from outside. Example: government spending on hospitals — this adds to firms' revenues without coming from current household spending.

### Extended Response Guides

**6.** (Australia's Mixed Economy — 6 mark guide)

Structure:
- Para 1: Define market economy features — price mechanism, private ownership, profit motive.
  Advantage: efficiency, innovation.  Australia has this (private sector = ~80% of GDP).
- Para 2: Define planned economy features — central control, equity goal.
  Limitation: Soviet collapse shows problems with pure planning.
- Para 3: How Australia combines both:
  Government provides: Medicare, Centrelink, public education, NDIS, defence
  Government regulates: ACCC, ASIC, environmental law
  Private sector still dominant: firms decide production, prices set by markets
- Para 4: Evaluation — not perfectly "combining the best of both."
  Market failures still exist (housing affordability, inequality).
  But the mixed approach has delivered Australia 30+ years of continuous growth.
  Conclude: statement is broadly accurate — mixed economy is pragmatic, not perfect.

**7.** (PPF and Growth — 8 mark guide)

Must include a clearly labelled PPF diagram.

- Movement ALONG the PPF: economy is already efficient; reallocation of resources from one
  good to another. Example: more capital goods, less consumer goods. Does NOT represent
  growth — total productive capacity unchanged. Involves opportunity cost.
- Shift of PPF OUTWARD: represents actual economic growth — increase in productive capacity.
  Causes: technology, capital investment, education, population growth, new resources.
- Draw "before" PPF and "after" (shifted out) PPF.
- Explain: a point previously unattainable (outside old PPF) becomes attainable — economy
  can now produce more of BOTH goods simultaneously.
- Link to living standards: outward shift → more goods available per person → improved wellbeing.

**8.** (Price Mechanism and Market Failure — 8 mark guide)

Part 1 — Price mechanism:
- High price signals: profitable to produce → firms increase supply; consumers economise
- Low price signals: over-supplied → producers cut back; consumers buy more
- Prices coordinate millions of decentralised decisions without central control
- Example: price of a resource (timber) rises after a natural disaster → logging companies
  have incentive to supply more; builders use alternatives

Part 2 — Market failure (choose ONE):
Best choice: negative externality (pollution)
- Factory produces steel, generates air pollution
- Factory only counts its private costs (wages, materials)
- Social cost (health damage to nearby residents, environmental harm) is IGNORED
- Because pollution is not priced, the factory over-produces → too much pollution
- Market produces MORE than the socially optimal quantity at a LOWER price than is socially optimal

Government response:
- **Carbon tax / pollution tax (Pigouvian tax):** adds the external cost to the price
  → raises cost of production → output falls toward socially optimal level
- **Regulation:** set maximum emission levels (cap pollution)
- **Cap and trade:** issue limited pollution permits; firms can trade them → efficient
- Evaluate: taxes most efficient; regulation easiest to enforce; each has limitations.

# SUMMARY — KEY CONCEPTS AT A GLANCE

```
THE ECONOMIC PROBLEM:
  Scarcity → need to choose → opportunity cost
  (wants unlimited, resources limited)

THREE QUESTIONS: What? How? For Whom?

FACTORS OF PRODUCTION:
  Land (rent) | Labour (wages) | Capital (interest) | Enterprise (profit)

ECONOMIC SYSTEMS:
  Traditional | Market (prices decide) | Planned (government decides) | Mixed

PPF:
  On PPF = efficient | Inside = inefficient | Outside = unattainable
  Movement along = reallocation | Shift out = economic growth
  Concave = Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost

CIRCULAR FLOW:
  Households ↔ Firms (via product markets and factor markets)
  Leakages: S + T + M     Injections: I + G + X
  Equilibrium: S + T + M = I + G + X

DEMAND: Price ↑ → Qd ↓    (inverse relationship, ceteris paribus)
SUPPLY: Price ↑ → Qs ↑    (positive relationship, ceteris paribus)
EQUILIBRIUM: Qd = Qs at the market-clearing price

POSITIVE vs NORMATIVE:
  Positive = facts (what IS)     Normative = opinions (what OUGHT TO BE)

ECONOMIC GOALS:
  Growth | Low unemployment | Low inflation | External stability | Equity
  Goals often TRADE OFF against each other (Phillips Curve)

MARKET FAILURE:
  Public goods | Externalities | Information failure | Market power
  → justifies government intervention
```
