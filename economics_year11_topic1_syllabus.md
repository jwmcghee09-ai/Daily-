# SECTION 1: THE ECONOMIC PROBLEM

---

## 1.1 Scarcity

> **DEFINITION — Scarcity:** The fundamental economic problem that arises because human wants
> are unlimited but the resources available to satisfy those wants are limited (finite).

Scarcity is a **permanent** condition — it has always existed and always will.
It affects every individual, business, and government regardless of how wealthy they are.

**Why scarcity exists:**
- Resources (land, labour, capital, enterprise) are limited in quantity
- Human wants are unlimited — satisfying one want creates new ones
- This mismatch means choices must be made

> **DEFINITION — Resources (Productive Resources / Factors of Production):** The inputs used
> in the production of goods and services — land, labour, capital, and enterprise.

> **DEFINITION — Wants:** The desires people have for goods and services. Wants are unlimited
> and insatiable — satisfying one creates more.

> **DEFINITION — Needs:** The basic requirements essential for human survival, such as food,
> water, shelter, and clothing.

**Scarcity vs Shortage — Key Distinction:**

| Term          | Meaning                                                   | Duration      |
|---------------|-----------------------------------------------------------|---------------|
| **Scarcity**  | Permanent gap between unlimited wants and limited resources | Always exists |
| **Shortage**  | Temporary situation where supply is less than demand at the current price | Temporary     |

**Example of scarcity:**
A government has a budget of $10 billion. It could spend it on hospitals, schools, roads,
defence, or welfare — but not all of them at once. This is scarcity in action.

> **DEFINITION — Free Goods:** Goods available in unlimited quantity at zero opportunity cost
> to society (e.g. sunlight, fresh air in most places). They do not require a choice to be made.

> **DEFINITION — Economic Goods:** Goods that are scarce — they require resources to produce
> and involve an opportunity cost. Most goods and services are economic goods.

---

## 1.2 Opportunity Cost

> **DEFINITION — Opportunity Cost:** The value of the next best alternative that must be
> forgone (given up) when a choice is made.

This is the **most important concept in economics**. Every choice has an opportunity cost
because resources are scarce — using them one way means they cannot be used another way.

**Key points:**
- Opportunity cost is specifically the **BEST alternative given up** — not just any alternative
- It applies to individuals, businesses, governments, and entire economies
- Opportunity cost is often **implicit** (not paid in money) — e.g. time has an opportunity cost

**Examples at different levels:**

| Level          | Choice Made                          | Opportunity Cost                        |
|----------------|--------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| Individual     | Spend Saturday studying              | The income from working (e.g. $80 shift)|
| Business       | Build a new factory                  | The investment returns from shares      |
| Government     | Build a new hospital ($500m)         | The roads/schools that $500m could build|
| Economy (PPF)  | Produce more consumer goods          | The capital goods that must be given up |

**The opportunity cost of a university degree:**
The explicit cost = tuition fees ($12,000/year)
The opportunity cost = the full-time salary forgone while studying ($60,000/year)
The TOTAL economic cost = $72,000/year — far more than just tuition

> **DEFINITION — Trade-off:** The situation that arises when choosing one option means
> accepting less of something else. All choices involve trade-offs because of scarcity.

---

## 1.3 Specialisation and Exchange

> **DEFINITION — Specialisation:** When individuals, firms, regions, or countries focus on
> producing the goods and services they are best at producing (have a comparative advantage in).

> **DEFINITION — Division of Labour:** A form of specialisation where the production process
> is broken into separate tasks, each performed by a different worker.

**Why specialisation increases output:**
- Workers become more skilled at their specific task (practice effect)
- No time lost switching between tasks
- Allows use of specialised machinery
- Workers can be matched to tasks they are best suited for

**Example — Car production:**
Without specialisation: one worker builds an entire car → 1 car per week
With specialisation: 100 workers each do one task → 500 cars per week

> **DEFINITION — Exchange:** The trading of goods and services between parties.
> Specialisation requires exchange — if you only produce one thing, you must trade for others.

> **DEFINITION — Barter:** Exchange of goods and services without using money.
> Barter requires a "double coincidence of wants" — both parties must want what the other has.

> **DEFINITION — Money:** Anything generally accepted as a medium of exchange.
> Money solves the double coincidence of wants problem and enables specialisation at large scale.

**Advantages of specialisation:**
- Higher output from same quantity of resources
- Lower costs (economies of scale)
- Higher living standards through increased production
- Development of skills and expertise

**Disadvantages of specialisation:**
- Workers become dependent on others for most goods
- Repetitive work can be boring and deskilling
- Disruption if one part of the chain breaks down
- Regions/countries become vulnerable if their specialisation becomes obsolete

> **DEFINITION — Comparative Advantage:** The ability of an individual, firm, or country to
> produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than others. This is the basis for all trade.

**Absolute Advantage vs Comparative Advantage:**

| Term                     | Definition                                                         |
|--------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Absolute advantage**   | Producing more of a good with the same resources as another producer |
| **Comparative advantage**| Producing a good at a lower opportunity cost than another producer |

Even if one country is better at producing EVERYTHING (absolute advantage), trade still
benefits both countries if each specialises in what it has a comparative advantage in.

## 1.4 The Factors of Production

> **DEFINITION — Factors of Production:** The four types of productive resources used to
> produce goods and services: Land, Labour, Capital, and Enterprise.

### Land

> **DEFINITION — Land:** All natural resources used in production — not just soil, but
> everything provided by nature including minerals, forests, water, fisheries, and the
> electromagnetic spectrum.

- **Reward for land:** Rent
- Land is fixed in total quantity (you can't make more land)
- Quality and productivity of land can be improved (irrigation, fertiliser)
- Examples: farmland, coal deposits, rivers used for hydropower, oil reserves, beaches

### Labour

> **DEFINITION — Labour:** The physical and mental effort of human beings used in the
> production of goods and services.

- **Reward for labour:** Wages and salaries
- Quantity of labour = size of the workforce (number of workers × hours worked)
- Quality of labour can be improved through education and training (human capital)
- Examples: factory workers, doctors, teachers, truck drivers, software engineers

> **DEFINITION — Human Capital:** The skills, knowledge, experience, and health embodied in
> workers as a result of education, training, and experience. Investment in human capital
> increases the productivity of labour.

### Capital

> **DEFINITION — Capital (Economic Capital / Physical Capital):** Man-made resources used
> in the production of other goods and services. Capital is NOT money.

- **Reward for capital:** Interest
- Capital is a produced factor of production — it must be made using other resources
- Examples: machinery, factories, tools, computers, roads, bridges, equipment

> **IMPORTANT:** Money is NOT a factor of production. Money is used to purchase factors of
> production, but money itself does not produce anything.

> **DEFINITION — Capital Goods:** Goods used to produce other goods (not for direct
> consumption). Examples: factory machines, tractors, construction cranes.

> **DEFINITION — Consumer Goods:** Goods used directly by consumers to satisfy wants.
> Examples: food, clothing, smartphones, furniture.

> **DEFINITION — Capital Accumulation (Investment):** The process of increasing the stock
> of capital goods in an economy. Investment in capital leads to economic growth.

### Enterprise

> **DEFINITION — Enterprise (Entrepreneurship):** The human skill of organising the other
> three factors of production (land, labour, capital) to produce goods and services, while
> taking on the financial risk of the business.

- **Reward for enterprise:** Profit (or loss if unsuccessful)
- The entrepreneur innovates, makes decisions, takes risks
- Examples: Gina Rinehart (mining), Elon Musk (Tesla/SpaceX), a local café owner

**Summary Table:**

| Factor       | Definition                    | Reward   | Examples                          |
|--------------|-------------------------------|----------|-----------------------------------|
| Land         | All natural resources          | Rent     | Soil, oil, water, forests         |
| Labour       | Human physical/mental effort   | Wages    | Workers, professionals            |
| Capital      | Man-made productive tools      | Interest | Machines, factories, roads        |
| Enterprise   | Organising + risk-taking       | Profit   | Entrepreneurs                     |

## 1.5 The Three Fundamental Economic Questions

Because of scarcity, every society must answer these three questions:

> **WHAT to produce?** — Which goods and services should be produced, and in what quantities?
> (e.g. more hospitals or more roads? More cars or more food?)

> **HOW to produce?** — Which combination of resources and production methods should be used?
> (e.g. labour-intensive or capital-intensive? Which technology?)

> **FOR WHOM to produce?** — Who receives the goods and services produced?
> (e.g. distributed equally? Based on income? Based on need?)

Different economic systems answer these questions differently.

## 1.6 The Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)

> **DEFINITION — Production Possibility Frontier (PPF):** A graph that shows all the
> maximum combinations of two goods (or categories of goods) that an economy can produce
> when all its resources are fully and efficiently employed.

Also called: Production Possibility Curve (PPC).

### Key Points on the PPF

```
  Consumer
  Goods     A
  (units) ●
     10   |  \
          |    \
      8   |  B  \
          |   ●  \
      6   |      \ C
          |       ●\
      4   |     X   \
          |   (●)    \ D
      2   |            ●
          |
      0   +─────────────●── Capital Goods
          0   2   4   6  8   10

Points ON the PPF (A, B, C, D): PRODUCTIVELY EFFICIENT
Point INSIDE the PPF (X):       PRODUCTIVELY INEFFICIENT
Points OUTSIDE the PPF:         CURRENTLY UNATTAINABLE
```

> **DEFINITION — Productive Efficiency:** Producing the maximum possible output from
> available resources. Represented by any point ON the PPF.

> **DEFINITION — Productive Inefficiency:** Producing less than maximum output — some
> resources are unemployed, wasted, or misallocated. Represented by any point INSIDE the PPF.

> **DEFINITION — Unattainable Production:** Output levels beyond what is currently possible
> given existing resources and technology. Represented by points OUTSIDE the PPF.

### The Shape — Why the PPF is Concave (Bowed Outward)

> **DEFINITION — Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost:** As an economy produces more of one
> good, the opportunity cost of each additional unit (in terms of the other good forgone)
> increases. This is because resources are not perfectly substitutable between uses.

**Why this happens:**
Resources are specialised — they are not equally good at producing both goods.
- When you first shift resources from Good B to Good A, you move the resources MOST suited to A → big gain in A, small loss in B
- As you shift more, you must move resources LESS suited to A → smaller gain in A, larger loss in B
- Eventually you move resources VERY poorly suited to A → tiny gain in A, massive loss in B

This produces the bowed-outward (concave) shape.

**A straight-line PPF** would imply constant opportunity cost — resources are perfectly
substitutable. This rarely occurs but is used as a simplification.

### Reading Opportunity Cost from the PPF

```
Moving from point B (8 consumer, 2 capital)
         to point C (6 consumer, 5 capital):

Gain: 3 units of capital goods
Give up: 2 units of consumer goods

Opportunity cost of 3 capital goods = 2 consumer goods
Opportunity cost of 1 capital good  = 2/3 of a consumer good
```

### Shifts of the PPF

> **DEFINITION — Economic Growth:** An increase in the productive capacity of an economy,
> shown by an outward shift of the PPF.

**Causes of an outward shift (economic growth):**
1. **Improved technology** — same resources produce more output
2. **Increase in labour** — population growth, immigration, higher participation
3. **Capital accumulation** — more machines, factories, infrastructure
4. **Improvement in human capital** — better education, training, health
5. **Discovery of new natural resources** — new oil fields, mineral deposits

**Causes of an inward shift (economic decline):**
1. Natural disasters (destroy resources)
2. War (destroys capital, kills workers)
3. Emigration (reduces labour force)
4. Environmental degradation (destroys land quality)

**Biased (asymmetric) shift:**
If technology improves in one sector only, the PPF shifts outward on that axis only.

```
Consumer   |         ← technology improves only in consumer goods sector
Goods      |    new /
           |   PPF /
           |     // old PPF
           |    //
           |   //
           +──────────── Capital Goods

Y-intercept rises (more consumer goods possible)
X-intercept unchanged (no change in capital goods capacity)
```

### Movement Along vs Shift of the PPF

| Type                   | What it means                          | Implication                          |
|------------------------|----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| **Movement along PPF** | Reallocation of existing resources     | Opportunity cost — more of one = less of other |
| **Shift of PPF**       | Change in productive capacity          | Economic growth (out) or decline (in)|

# SECTION 2: ECONOMIC SYSTEMS

---

## 2.1 What is an Economic System?

> **DEFINITION — Economic System:** The way in which a society organises and allocates
> its scarce resources to answer the three fundamental economic questions (What? How? For Whom?)

---

## 2.2 Traditional Economy

> **DEFINITION — Traditional Economy:** An economic system where decisions about production
> and distribution are based on customs, traditions, and cultural practices passed down
> through generations.

**Characteristics:**
- Economic decisions follow long-established customs and rituals
- Production methods are inherited, not chosen
- Community or family-based rather than profit-driven
- Subsistence production (just enough to survive) is common
- Little economic change over time

**Examples:** Indigenous Australian communities (pre-colonisation), some remote African or Pacific Island communities

**Advantages:**
- Strong social cohesion and cultural identity
- Sustainable — low environmental impact
- Roles and expectations are clear

**Disadvantages:**
- Unable to adapt to change or new technology
- Low productivity and material living standards
- Cannot respond to growth in population needs

---

## 2.3 Market Economy (Free Market / Capitalism)

> **DEFINITION — Market Economy:** An economic system where decisions about production and
> distribution are made through the price mechanism — the interaction of supply and demand
> in markets — with resources privately owned.

> **DEFINITION — Price Mechanism:** The process by which changes in the price of goods and
> services coordinate the decisions of buyers and sellers, allocating resources to their
> most valued uses without central direction.

**Key features:**

| Feature                    | Explanation                                                      |
|----------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Private property rights**| Individuals own and control resources                           |
| **Price mechanism**        | Prices signal what to produce and in what quantities            |
| **Profit motive**          | Firms produce what is profitable → drives efficiency            |
| **Competition**            | Multiple firms compete → better quality, lower prices           |
| **Freedom of choice**      | Consumers buy what they want; workers choose where to work      |
| **Self-interest**          | Individuals and firms act in their own interest                 |

> **DEFINITION — The Invisible Hand (Adam Smith, 1776):** The idea that individuals pursuing
> their own self-interest in markets are guided, as if by an invisible hand, to promote the
> economic wellbeing of society as a whole — without intending to do so.

**Advantages of a market economy:**
- Efficient allocation of resources (price signals direct resources to most valued uses)
- Encourages innovation and entrepreneurship (profit reward)
- Wide variety of goods and services
- Economic freedom for individuals and firms
- Historical record of strong economic growth

**Disadvantages of a market economy:**
- Income inequality (wealth concentrates with successful producers)
- Market failures (some goods underprovided — e.g. defence, street lighting)
- Negative externalities ignored (e.g. pollution — no cost to polluter)
- No social safety net — unemployed, sick, elderly may not be provided for
- Short-term focus — may not invest in long-term public goods

**Examples:** United States (mostly), Hong Kong, Singapore

---

## 2.4 Planned (Command) Economy

> **DEFINITION — Planned Economy (Command Economy):** An economic system where the
> government (central authority) makes all decisions about what to produce, how to produce
> it, and how output is distributed among the population.

**Key features:**
- State (government) owns most or all productive resources
- Central planning authority sets production targets and prices
- No price mechanism — government decides allocation
- Profit motive replaced by government directives
- Little or no freedom of choice for individuals or firms

**Historical examples:** Soviet Union (1917–1991), North Korea, Cuba (partially), China (pre-1978 reforms)

**Advantages of a planned economy:**
- Can rapidly mobilise resources toward national goals (war, infrastructure)
- Can theoretically eliminate unemployment (state provides all jobs)
- More equal distribution of income and wealth
- Can prioritise social goods (healthcare, education) regardless of profitability
- Can plan for the long term without short-term profit pressure

**Disadvantages of a planned economy:**
- Lack of incentive — no profit motive → low efficiency and poor quality
- Information problem — central planners cannot process the information that millions of market prices convey automatically
- Chronic shortages and surpluses — without prices, supply and demand cannot balance
- Suppresses individual freedom and choice
- Historical track record of economic failure and low living standards
- Corruption and bureaucratic inefficiency

---

## 2.5 Mixed Economy

> **DEFINITION — Mixed Economy:** An economic system that combines elements of both the
> market economy and the planned economy — private markets allocate most resources, but
> the government intervenes to correct market failures, provide public goods, redistribute
> income, and stabilise the economy.

**The vast majority of modern economies are mixed economies**, including Australia, the UK,
the USA, Canada, France, and Japan — they differ only in the degree of government involvement.

### Government's Role in a Mixed Economy

> **DEFINITION — Government Intervention:** Actions taken by the government to influence
> the allocation of resources in the economy, either to correct market failures or to
> achieve social and economic objectives.

The government plays five main roles:

**1. Provision of public goods**
Provide goods the private market will not supply (defence, street lighting, public parks).

**2. Regulation of markets**
Set rules to ensure fair competition and prevent abuse (competition law, safety standards).

**3. Redistribution of income**
Tax higher-income earners and transfer funds to lower-income groups (welfare, Medicare).

**4. Correction of market failures**
Tax negative externalities (pollution), subsidise positive externalities (education).

**5. Macroeconomic stabilisation**
Use fiscal policy (spending/taxes) and monetary policy (interest rates) to manage the economy.

### Australia's Mixed Economy

Australia is primarily a **market economy** with significant government involvement:

| Sector        | Examples in Australia                                           |
|---------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Private sector| Most businesses (retail, manufacturing, mining, finance)        |
| Government provision | Medicare, public schools, TAFE, defence, roads, welfare |
| Government regulation| ACCC (competition), ASIC (finance), EPA (environment)  |
| Monetary policy| Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) sets interest rates           |
| Fiscal policy | Federal and state government budgets (spending and taxation)   |

---

## 2.6 Resource Allocation

> **DEFINITION — Resource Allocation:** The process of distributing scarce productive
> resources among competing uses to produce goods and services.

**In a market economy:** resources are allocated through the price mechanism
**In a planned economy:** resources are allocated by government decisions
**In a mixed economy:** combination of both

> **DEFINITION — Allocative Efficiency:** When resources are allocated to produce the
> combination of goods and services that best satisfies the wants of society — no resources
> are wasted, and it's impossible to make someone better off without making someone else worse off.

## 3.1 Markets

> **DEFINITION — Market:** Any arrangement (physical or virtual) that brings buyers and
> sellers together to exchange goods, services, or resources, determining price and quantity.

Markets do NOT need to be physical places — the internet, phone, stock exchange are all markets.

**Types of markets:**

| Market Type          | What is Traded                   | Examples                        |
|----------------------|----------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Product markets**  | Final goods and services         | Supermarkets, car dealerships   |
| **Factor markets**   | Factors of production            | Labour market, property market  |
| **Financial markets**| Financial assets (shares, bonds) | ASX (Australian Securities Exchange)|
| **Foreign exchange** | Currencies                       | AUD/USD market                  |

## 3.2 Demand

> **DEFINITION — Demand:** The quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and
> able to purchase at each possible price over a given time period, all else being equal
> (ceteris paribus).

**Note:** Demand requires BOTH willingness AND ability to pay. Wanting something without being
able to pay is not demand in the economic sense.

### The Law of Demand

> **DEFINITION — Law of Demand:** As the price of a good rises, the quantity demanded falls;
> as the price falls, the quantity demanded rises — ceteris paribus (all else equal).

**Why the law of demand holds — two effects:**

> **DEFINITION — Substitution Effect:** When the price of a good rises, consumers switch to
> cheaper substitute goods, reducing quantity demanded of the original good.

> **DEFINITION — Income Effect:** When the price of a good rises, consumers' real purchasing
> power falls (their income buys less), reducing the quantity demanded of most goods.

### The Demand Curve

```
Price ($)
    |
 10 |●
    |  \
  8 |   ●
    |    \
  6 |     ●
    |      \
  4 |       ●
    |        \
  2 |         ●
    |          \
    +────────────────── Quantity Demanded (units)
     0   5  10  15  20
```

The demand curve is **downward sloping** — showing the inverse (negative) relationship
between price and quantity demanded.

### Movement Along vs Shift of the Demand Curve

> **DEFINITION — Change in Quantity Demanded:** A movement ALONG the demand curve caused
> by a change in the PRICE of the good itself. The demand curve does not move.

> **DEFINITION — Change in Demand (Demand Shift):** A movement OF the entire demand curve
> left or right, caused by a change in a NON-PRICE factor (demand determinant).

```
Increase in demand:         Decrease in demand:
Price                       Price
  |     D₁  D₂               |   D₂  D₁
  |      |  /                 |    \  |
  |      | /                  |     \ |
  |      |/ ← shifts right    |      \| ← shifts left
  +──────────── Qty           +──────────── Qty
```

### Non-Price Determinants of Demand (Demand Shifters)

| Determinant                        | Effect on Demand                                   | Shift Direction |
|------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Income rises** (normal good)     | Can afford more → demand rises                     | Right           |
| **Income rises** (inferior good)   | Switch to better alternatives → demand falls       | Left            |
| **Price of substitute rises**      | Good becomes relatively cheaper → demand rises     | Right           |
| **Price of complement falls**      | Both used together, lower price → demand rises     | Right           |
| **Tastes/preferences increase**    | More desirable → demand rises                      | Right           |
| **Population increases**           | More potential buyers → demand rises               | Right           |
| **Expected future price rises**    | Buy now before price rises → demand rises today    | Right           |

> **DEFINITION — Normal Good:** A good for which demand increases as consumer income rises.
> Examples: restaurants, cars, holidays.

> **DEFINITION — Inferior Good:** A good for which demand decreases as consumer income rises
> (consumers switch to better alternatives). Examples: instant noodles, second-hand clothing.

> **DEFINITION — Substitute Goods:** Goods that can replace one another in consumption —
> when the price of one rises, demand for the other rises. Examples: butter/margarine, Coke/Pepsi.

> **DEFINITION — Complementary Goods:** Goods that are consumed together — when the price
> of one falls, demand for the other rises. Examples: cars/petrol, printers/ink cartridges.

## 3.3 Supply

> **DEFINITION — Supply:** The quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and
> able to offer for sale at each possible price over a given time period, ceteris paribus.

### The Law of Supply

> **DEFINITION — Law of Supply:** As the price of a good rises, the quantity supplied rises;
> as the price falls, the quantity supplied falls — ceteris paribus.

**Why the law of supply holds:**
- Higher price → higher revenue per unit → more profitable → incentive to produce more
- Higher price attracts new firms into the market (new entrants)
- Lower price → less profitable or unprofitable → reduce production

### The Supply Curve

```
Price ($)
    |            /
 10 |          ●/
    |         /
  8 |        ●
    |       /
  6 |      ●
    |     /
  4 |    ●
    |   /
  2 |  ●
    | /
    +──────────────── Quantity Supplied (units)
     0   5  10  15  20
```

The supply curve is **upward sloping** — showing the positive relationship between price
and quantity supplied.

### Movement Along vs Shift of the Supply Curve

> **DEFINITION — Change in Quantity Supplied:** A movement ALONG the supply curve caused
> by a change in the PRICE of the good itself.

> **DEFINITION — Change in Supply (Supply Shift):** A movement OF the entire supply curve
> left or right, caused by a change in a NON-PRICE factor (supply determinant).

```
Increase in supply:         Decrease in supply:
Price                       Price
  |  S₁  S₂                 |    S₂ S₁
  |   \   \                  |     \  \
  |    \   \← shifts right   |      \  \← shifts left
  |     \   \                |       \  \
  +──────────── Qty          +──────────── Qty
```

### Non-Price Determinants of Supply (Supply Shifters)

| Determinant                         | Effect on Supply                                  | Shift Direction |
|-------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Cost of production falls**        | More profitable at every price → supply rises     | Right           |
| **Technology improves**             | Can produce more cheaply → supply rises           | Right           |
| **Number of producers increases**   | More firms = more supply                          | Right           |
| **Input prices fall** (wages, materials) | Lower costs → supply rises                  | Right           |
| **Government subsidy granted**      | Lowers cost → supply rises                        | Right           |
| **Government tax imposed**          | Raises cost → supply falls                        | Left            |
| **Expected future price rises**     | Withhold supply now → supply falls today          | Left            |
| **Favourable weather** (agriculture)| Increases crop yield → supply rises               | Right           |

> **DEFINITION — Subsidy:** A payment made by the government to producers to lower their
> costs of production, encouraging greater output. Shifts supply curve right.

> **DEFINITION — Indirect Tax:** A tax placed on the production or sale of a good (e.g. GST),
> raising production costs and shifting the supply curve left.

## 3.4 Market Equilibrium

> **DEFINITION — Market Equilibrium:** The price and quantity at which the quantity demanded
> equals the quantity supplied — the market "clears" with no surplus or shortage.

> **DEFINITION — Equilibrium Price:** The price at which quantity demanded equals quantity
> supplied. Also called the market-clearing price.

> **DEFINITION — Equilibrium Quantity:** The quantity bought and sold at the equilibrium price.

```
Price ($)      S
    |         /
  8 |        /
    |       /
  6 |──────×──────────── equilibrium (P* = $6, Q* = 200)
    |      /\
  4 |     /  \
    |    /    \  D
    |   /
    +────────────────── Quantity
         200
```

### Surplus (Excess Supply)

> **DEFINITION — Surplus (Excess Supply):** When quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded
> at the current price (price is ABOVE equilibrium). Sellers cannot sell all they produce.

**Mechanism:** Unsold stock accumulates → firms lower price → Qd rises, Qs falls → equilibrium restored

```
At price $8 (above equilibrium):
  Qs = 250,   Qd = 150
  Surplus = 100 units → price falls → equilibrium restored
```

### Shortage (Excess Demand)

> **DEFINITION — Shortage (Excess Demand):** When quantity demanded exceeds quantity
> supplied at the current price (price is BELOW equilibrium). Consumers cannot get all they want.

**Mechanism:** Unsatisfied buyers bid up price → Qs rises, Qd falls → equilibrium restored

```
At price $4 (below equilibrium):
  Qd = 250,   Qs = 150
  Shortage = 100 units → price rises → equilibrium restored
```

## 3.5 Changes in Market Equilibrium

When demand or supply changes, equilibrium price and quantity change.

### Change in Demand — Worked Example

**Scenario:** Consumer incomes rise → demand for new cars increases

```
Effect: Demand curve shifts RIGHT (increase in demand)

Price      S
    |     /
 P₂ |────/────×₂         ← new equilibrium: higher price, higher quantity
    |   /   / \
 P₁ |──/──×₁   \
    | /   / \   \
    |/   /   \   D₂
    |   /    D₁
    +────────────────── Quantity
        Q₁   Q₂

Result: Price rises (P₁ → P₂), Quantity rises (Q₁ → Q₂)
```

**Rule: Demand increases → price rises AND quantity rises**
**Rule: Demand decreases → price falls AND quantity falls**

### Change in Supply — Worked Example

**Scenario:** A new, cheaper production technology is developed → supply of smartphones increases

```
Effect: Supply curve shifts RIGHT (increase in supply)

Price     S₁  S₂
    |     /  /
 P₁ |────×₁ /            ← old equilibrium
    |   / \ /
 P₂ |──/──×₂             ← new equilibrium: lower price, higher quantity
    | /   /\
    |/   /  D
    +────────────────── Quantity
       Q₁  Q₂

Result: Price falls (P₁ → P₂), Quantity rises (Q₁ → Q₂)
```

**Rule: Supply increases → price falls, quantity rises**
**Rule: Supply decreases → price rises, quantity falls**

### Summary Table — Effects of Changes

| Change             | Price    | Quantity |
|--------------------|----------|----------|
| Demand increases   | Rises    | Rises    |
| Demand decreases   | Falls    | Falls    |
| Supply increases   | Falls    | Rises    |
| Supply decreases   | Rises    | Falls    |

---

## 3.6 The Price Mechanism

> **DEFINITION — Price Mechanism:** The system by which the forces of supply and demand
> interact to determine the price and quantity of goods and services exchanged in markets.
> Prices act as signals that coordinate economic decisions.

**Three functions of prices:**

> **DEFINITION — Signalling Function of Prices:** Prices transmit information about relative
> scarcity and value to buyers and sellers. A rising price signals that a good is relatively
> more scarce or desirable; a falling price signals the opposite.

> **DEFINITION — Incentive Function of Prices:** Prices create incentives for producers to
> supply goods (high price = high profit = incentive to produce more) and for consumers to
> economise (high price = incentive to buy less or find substitutes).

> **DEFINITION — Rationing Function of Prices:** Prices ration scarce goods among competing
> buyers — those willing and able to pay the market price get the good; others do not.

---

## 3.7 Market Failure

> **DEFINITION — Market Failure:** A situation where the free market fails to allocate
> resources efficiently, leading to an outcome that does not maximise social welfare.
> Market failure justifies government intervention.

### 1. Public Goods

> **DEFINITION — Public Good:** A good that is non-rival and non-excludable, which the
> private market will not provide in sufficient quantity.

> **DEFINITION — Non-rival:** Consumption by one person does not reduce the amount
> available to others (e.g. national defence, street lighting).

> **DEFINITION — Non-excludable:** It is impossible to prevent non-payers from consuming
> the good once it is provided.

> **DEFINITION — Free Rider Problem:** When individuals can benefit from a public good
> without paying for it, creating an incentive to not pay — leading to underprovision by
> private markets. The government must provide the good using tax revenue.

Examples: National defence, street lights, lighthouses, public fireworks, flood levees

### 2. Externalities

> **DEFINITION — Externality:** A cost or benefit that falls on third parties who are not
> involved in a market transaction — a "spillover" effect not captured by the market price.

> **DEFINITION — Negative Externality:** A cost imposed on third parties as a result of
> a market transaction. The social cost exceeds the private cost.
> Example: Air pollution from a factory harms nearby residents.

> **DEFINITION — Positive Externality:** A benefit received by third parties as a result
> of a market transaction. The social benefit exceeds the private benefit.
> Example: Getting vaccinated protects not just you but people you contact.

**Result of negative externalities:** Market over-produces (ignores social costs)
**Result of positive externalities:** Market under-produces (ignores social benefits)

**Government responses:**
- Tax negative externalities (Pigouvian tax / carbon tax) → raises private cost → less produced
- Subsidise positive externalities → lowers cost → more produced
- Regulate (set legal limits on pollution)

### 3. Information Failure

> **DEFINITION — Information Failure (Asymmetric Information):** A market failure that
> occurs when one party in a transaction has more or better information than the other,
> leading to inefficient outcomes.

Examples:
- Used car market: seller knows the car's true condition; buyer does not
- Health insurance: insured person knows their health risk; insurer does not
- Financial products: complex products that consumers cannot properly evaluate

### 4. Market Power (Monopoly)

> **DEFINITION — Monopoly:** A market structure where a single firm is the only supplier
> of a good or service, giving it market power to set prices above competitive levels.

> **DEFINITION — Market Power:** The ability of a firm to influence the price of a good
> or service in a market, due to lack of competition.

Result: Higher prices, lower output, less consumer choice → government may regulate or break up monopolies

# SECTION 4: THE CIRCULAR FLOW OF INCOME

---

## 4.1 What is the Circular Flow?

> **DEFINITION — Circular Flow of Income:** A model of the economy that shows how income,
> expenditure, and output flow between the sectors of the economy in a continuous cycle.

The model shows that:
- Every purchase by a buyer = income for a seller
- Every payment for a resource = income for a resource owner
- Total income = Total output = Total expenditure (in equilibrium)

---

## 4.2 Two-Sector Model (Households and Firms)

The simplest version involves only two sectors:

> **DEFINITION — Households:** The sector that owns all factors of production and purchases
> goods and services. Households supply resources to firms in exchange for income.

> **DEFINITION — Firms:** The sector that uses factors of production to produce goods and
> services. Firms pay households for resources and receive payment for output.

```
                    PRODUCT MARKETS
                 ┌────────────────────┐
  Goods &        │                    │   Consumer
  Services ─────►│                    │◄─ Spending
                 │                    │
   FIRMS         │                    │   HOUSEHOLDS
                 │                    │
  Wages/Rent/   ◄│                    │►  Labour/Land/
  Profit/Interest│                    │   Capital/Enterprise
                 │                    │
                 └────────────────────┘
                    FACTOR MARKETS
```

**Two flows:**

> **DEFINITION — Real Flow:** The flow of actual resources (labour, land, capital, enterprise)
> from households to firms, and goods and services back from firms to households.

> **DEFINITION — Money Flow:** The flow of money payments — wages, rent, interest, profit
> from firms to households, and consumer expenditure flowing back from households to firms.

---

## 4.3 Five-Sector Model

The full model adds three more sectors:

> **DEFINITION — Financial Sector:** Banks and other financial institutions that collect
> savings from households and make them available for investment by firms. Connects saving (leakage) to investment (injection).

> **DEFINITION — Government Sector:** Federal, state, and local governments that collect
> taxes (leakage) and make government expenditure on goods, services, and transfers (injection).

> **DEFINITION — Overseas Sector (Foreign Sector):** The rest of the world. Australians
> spend on imports (leakage); foreigners spend on Australian exports (injection).

**The Five Sectors:**
1. Households
2. Firms
3. Financial sector (banks)
4. Government
5. Overseas sector

---

## 4.4 Leakages and Injections

> **DEFINITION — Leakage (Withdrawal):** A flow of income out of the circular flow of income
> — money that is not passed directly from households to domestic firms as expenditure.

The three leakages are:

| Leakage      | Definition                                                             |
|--------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Saving (S)**   | Household income not spent on domestic goods — deposited in banks  |
| **Taxation (T)** | Income taken by the government — reduces disposable income         |
| **Imports (M)**  | Spending on foreign-produced goods — income goes overseas          |

> **DEFINITION — Injection:** A flow of income INTO the circular flow from outside the
> household-firm relationship — adding to the total spending in the economy.

The three injections are:

| Injection              | Definition                                                        |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Investment (I)**     | Business spending on capital goods — funded by financial sector savings |
| **Government spending (G)** | Government expenditure on goods, services, and transfers   |
| **Exports (X)**        | Spending by foreigners on Australian goods — income flows IN     |

### Equilibrium Condition

> **DEFINITION — Macroeconomic Equilibrium:** The condition where total leakages equal
> total injections — the circular flow is stable and income is neither rising nor falling.

```
Equilibrium:    S + T + M  =  I + G + X

If I + G + X  >  S + T + M  →  Economy GROWS (income rises)
If I + G + X  <  S + T + M  →  Economy CONTRACTS (income falls)
```

**Example:**
If Australian households save $200b, pay $400b in taxes, and spend $150b on imports:
S + T + M = $750b (leakages)
For equilibrium: Investment + Government spending + Exports must also = $750b

---

## 4.5 National Income

> **DEFINITION — National Income (GDP — Gross Domestic Product):** The total market value
> of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given time period (usually one year).

**Three equivalent approaches to measuring GDP:**

| Approach            | Method                                            | Formula                |
|---------------------|---------------------------------------------------|------------------------|
| **Expenditure**     | Add up all spending on final goods/services       | GDP = C + I + G + (X−M)|
| **Income**          | Add up all incomes earned (wages + rent + interest + profit) | GDP = all factor incomes |
| **Output/Production**| Add up value added at each stage of production  | GDP = sum of value added|

**The Expenditure Approach — GDP Formula:**

> **GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)**

Where:
- **C** = Household consumption expenditure (largest component, ~55% of GDP)
- **I** = Business investment expenditure (capital goods, construction, inventories)
- **G** = Government expenditure on goods and services (NOT transfers like welfare)
- **X** = Exports (spending by foreigners on Australian goods)
- **M** = Imports (Australian spending on foreign goods — subtracted as it's not Australian production)
- **(X − M)** = Net exports (can be positive or negative)

> **DEFINITION — Gross Domestic Product (GDP):** The total market value of all final goods
> and services produced within a country's borders in a given year, regardless of who owns the resources.

> **DEFINITION — Gross National Product (GNP):** The total market value of all goods and
> services produced by a country's residents, regardless of where they are located.
> GNP = GDP + income earned by residents overseas − income earned by foreigners domestically.

> **DEFINITION — Final Goods and Services:** Goods and services sold to their ultimate end
> user. Intermediate goods (inputs used to make other goods) are NOT counted to avoid double-counting.

# SECTION 5: ECONOMIC OBJECTIVES AND LIVING STANDARDS

---

## 5.1 Australia's Economic Objectives

> **DEFINITION — Economic Objectives:** The goals that a government seeks to achieve in
> managing the economy. In Australia, the main objectives are:

| Objective                            | Target / Indicator                              |
|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| **Strong and sustainable economic growth** | Real GDP growth of ~3% per year           |
| **Full employment (low unemployment)** | Unemployment rate of ~4–5%                  |
| **Price stability (low inflation)**  | CPI inflation of 2–3% per year (RBA target)   |
| **External stability**               | Manageable current account deficit            |
| **Equitable distribution of income** | Reducing poverty and income inequality        |
| **Environmental sustainability**     | Protecting the natural environment for future generations |

---

## 5.2 Economic Growth

> **DEFINITION — Economic Growth:** An increase in the real value of goods and services
> produced in the economy over time — measured as the percentage change in real GDP.

> **DEFINITION — Real GDP:** GDP adjusted for inflation — measures actual changes in output
> volume rather than changes in prices.

> **DEFINITION — Nominal GDP:** GDP measured at current prices — NOT adjusted for inflation.

```
Real GDP growth rate = [(GDP this year − GDP last year) / GDP last year] × 100
```

**Benefits of economic growth:**
- Higher living standards (more goods and services available)
- Lower unemployment (more jobs created)
- More tax revenue → government can spend more on services
- Business confidence and investment increase

**Costs of economic growth:**
- Inflation (too-rapid growth creates demand-pull inflation)
- Environmental degradation (more production = more pollution)
- Income inequality can worsen if benefits concentrated at top
- Depletion of natural resources

> **DEFINITION — Sustainable Economic Growth:** Growth that can be maintained over the long
> term without causing inflation, environmental damage, or resource depletion.

---

## 5.3 Employment and Unemployment

> **DEFINITION — Labour Force:** All people aged 15 and over who are either employed or
> actively seeking employment. The labour force = Employed + Unemployed.

> **DEFINITION — Unemployment:** When people who are willing and able to work and are
> actively seeking employment cannot find a job.

> **DEFINITION — Unemployment Rate:** The percentage of the labour force that is unemployed.

```
Unemployment rate = (Number unemployed / Labour force) × 100
```

> **DEFINITION — Participation Rate (Labour Force Participation Rate):** The percentage of
> the working-age population (15+) that is in the labour force (employed or seeking work).

```
Participation rate = (Labour force / Working-age population) × 100
```

### Types of Unemployment

> **DEFINITION — Cyclical Unemployment (Demand-Deficient Unemployment):** Unemployment
> caused by a downturn in the business cycle — insufficient demand for goods and services
> means firms need fewer workers. Rises during recessions; falls during booms.

> **DEFINITION — Structural Unemployment:** Unemployment caused by changes in the structure
> of the economy — workers' skills no longer match the available jobs due to technological
> change, industry decline, or globalisation.
> Example: coal miners unemployed due to shift to renewable energy.

> **DEFINITION — Frictional Unemployment:** Short-term unemployment that occurs when workers
> are between jobs — searching for new employment or transitioning between roles.
> Considered normal and unavoidable in a dynamic economy.

> **DEFINITION — Seasonal Unemployment:** Unemployment that occurs in certain seasons due
> to the seasonal nature of some industries.
> Example: ski resort workers unemployed in summer; farm workers unemployed off-season.

> **DEFINITION — Full Employment:** The level of employment where all those willing and able
> to work at the current wage rate can find employment. Allows for frictional and structural
> unemployment — typically around 4–5% in Australia.

> **DEFINITION — Natural Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU — Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of
> Unemployment):** The unemployment rate consistent with stable inflation — includes
> frictional and structural unemployment but NOT cyclical unemployment.

**Costs of unemployment:**
- Lost output (the economy produces less than potential)
- Lost income for unemployed individuals
- Psychological harm (stress, loss of identity)
- Increased government spending on welfare
- Loss of skills over time (unemployed workers' skills deteriorate)
- Social costs (increased crime, family breakdown)

---

## 5.4 Inflation

> **DEFINITION — Inflation:** A sustained increase in the general level of prices over time,
> resulting in a fall in the purchasing power of money.

> **DEFINITION — Deflation:** A sustained decrease in the general level of prices — generally
> harmful as it encourages consumers to delay purchases, reducing economic activity.

> **DEFINITION — Consumer Price Index (CPI):** A measure of inflation that tracks changes in
> the price of a representative "basket" of goods and services purchased by a typical
> Australian household. Published quarterly by the Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS).

```
Inflation rate = [(CPI this year − CPI last year) / CPI last year] × 100
```

> **DEFINITION — Purchasing Power:** The quantity of goods and services that a unit of
> currency (e.g. $1) can buy. Inflation reduces purchasing power.

### Types of Inflation

> **DEFINITION — Demand-Pull Inflation:** Inflation caused by excess demand in the economy —
> "too much money chasing too few goods." Occurs when the economy is growing rapidly.
> Associated with low unemployment and strong consumer spending.

> **DEFINITION — Cost-Push Inflation:** Inflation caused by rising costs of production
> (wages, raw materials, energy) that are passed on to consumers as higher prices.
> Example: oil price shock raises transport costs → prices rise across all sectors.

> **DEFINITION — Imported Inflation:** Inflation caused by rising prices of imported goods
> and raw materials, often due to exchange rate depreciation or global price increases.

**Costs of inflation:**
- Erodes the purchasing power of money (savings worth less)
- Distorts price signals (harder to make economic decisions)
- Reduces international competitiveness (Australian goods become more expensive)
- Hurts those on fixed incomes (pensioners, welfare recipients)
- Creates uncertainty → reduces business investment
- Redistributes wealth from creditors to debtors (loan repayments worth less)

**Benefits of low, stable inflation (2–3%):**
- Encourages spending (money loses value if held → spend now)
- Allows wages to adjust without requiring nominal cuts
- Provides buffer against deflation

---

## 5.5 External Stability

> **DEFINITION — External Stability:** The ability of Australia to meet its international
> financial obligations — involving management of the current account, foreign debt, and
> the exchange rate.

> **DEFINITION — Balance of Payments:** A record of all economic transactions between
> Australia and the rest of the world over a period of time.

> **DEFINITION — Current Account:** The component of the balance of payments that records
> trade in goods and services, income flows, and current transfers between Australia and
> the rest of the world.

> **DEFINITION — Current Account Deficit (CAD):** When Australia's payments to the rest of
> the world exceed its receipts — Australia imports more than it exports.

> **DEFINITION — Terms of Trade:** The ratio of export prices to import prices.
> A rise in the terms of trade means Australia gets more imports for each unit of exports.

---

## 5.6 Distribution of Income

> **DEFINITION — Income Distribution:** The way in which the total income of an economy is
> shared among individuals and households.

> **DEFINITION — Equitable Distribution of Income:** A distribution of income considered
> fair by society — not necessarily perfectly equal, but not excessively unequal.

> **DEFINITION — Gini Coefficient:** A statistical measure of income inequality ranging
> from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality — one person has all income).

> **DEFINITION — Poverty:** A condition where individuals or households lack sufficient
> income to meet basic needs or maintain a minimum acceptable standard of living.

> **DEFINITION — Absolute Poverty:** Lacking the income necessary for basic survival
> (food, shelter, clean water).

> **DEFINITION — Relative Poverty:** Having an income significantly below the average
> income in a society — unable to participate fully in normal social life.

**Government redistribution tools:**
- Progressive income tax (higher earners pay a higher percentage)
- Transfer payments (welfare, Centrelink payments)
- Universal services (Medicare, public education)
- Minimum wage

---

## 5.7 Trade-offs Between Economic Objectives

> **DEFINITION — Trade-off:** A situation where achieving more of one economic objective
> requires accepting less of another.

### The Phillips Curve — Inflation vs Unemployment

> **DEFINITION — Phillips Curve:** A graph showing the inverse (negative) relationship
> between inflation and unemployment — when unemployment is low, inflation tends to rise;
> when unemployment is high, inflation tends to fall.

```
Inflation (%)
    |
  6 |  ●  A (low unemployment, high inflation)
    |     \
  4 |      \  ●  B
    |          \
  2 |           \  ●  C (high unemployment, low inflation)
    |
    +────────────────────── Unemployment (%)
       3%    5%     7%

Moving from C to A: unemployment falls but inflation rises.
Moving from A to C: inflation falls but unemployment rises.
```

**Why:** Low unemployment → workers have bargaining power → wages rise → firms raise prices → inflation.
High unemployment → workers accept lower wages → cost pressure eases → inflation falls.

### Growth vs Inflation Trade-off
Rapid economic growth → more income → more spending → demand-pull inflation.
To control inflation → slow down growth → unemployment may rise.

### Growth vs Environment Trade-off
Economic growth often increases production → more resource use → environmental damage.
Environmental protection (emissions limits) → may constrain output and growth.

### Growth vs Equity Trade-off
Policies that maximise growth (lower taxes, less regulation) may increase inequality.
Policies that reduce inequality (higher taxes, more welfare) may reduce incentives to work and invest.

---

## 5.8 Living Standards

> **DEFINITION — Living Standards:** The level of material and non-material wellbeing
> experienced by individuals and households in an economy.

> **DEFINITION — Material Living Standards:** The quantity of goods, services, and income
> available to individuals and households — measured by GDP per capita, real wages, etc.

> **DEFINITION — Non-Material Living Standards:** Aspects of wellbeing not captured by
> income or consumption — including leisure time, health, education, safety, environmental
> quality, social connections, and political freedoms.

> **DEFINITION — GDP per Capita:** The total GDP of a country divided by its population.
> A commonly used (but imperfect) measure of average living standards.

```
GDP per capita = GDP / Population
```

**Limitations of GDP per capita as a measure of living standards:**
- Does not capture distribution (high GDP per capita could coexist with extreme inequality)
- Ignores non-material factors (leisure, health, environment, happiness)
- Does not account for unpaid work (e.g. caring, volunteering)
- Includes spending that doesn't improve wellbeing (e.g. crime cleanup, pollution remediation)
- Ignores the sustainability of growth

**Better measures of living standards:**
- Human Development Index (HDI): combines income, education, and life expectancy
- Genuine Progress Indicator (GPI): adjusts GDP for inequality, crime, pollution
- Gross National Happiness (GNH): Bhutan's measure of national wellbeing

---

## 5.9 Positive vs Normative Statements

> **DEFINITION — Positive Statement:** An objective, factual statement about the economy
> that can be tested and verified (or refuted) with evidence. Describes what IS.

> **DEFINITION — Normative Statement:** A subjective statement based on value judgements
> and opinions about what the economy OUGHT to be or what policies SHOULD be implemented.
> Cannot be tested empirically — involves values, not just facts.

| Statement                                              | Type      | Why                                   |
|--------------------------------------------------------|-----------|---------------------------------------|
| "Australia's unemployment rate is 4.1%"                | Positive  | Measurable, factual                   |
| "The government should increase welfare payments"      | Normative | "Should" = value judgement            |
| "Inflation is currently 3.5%"                          | Positive  | Factual, can be verified              |
| "Low inflation is more important than low unemployment"| Normative | Comparative value judgement           |
| "Raising the minimum wage reduces employment"          | Positive  | An empirical claim (debated, but testable) |
| "Australia should prioritise environmental protection" | Normative | Value judgement about priorities      |

| **Evaluate**  | Make a judgement — discuss advantages AND disadvantages, then conclude  |
| **Discuss**   | Present multiple viewpoints or aspects of an issue                      |

## Key Relationships to Know

```
Scarcity → Choices → Opportunity Cost → Trade-offs

Specialisation → Increased output → Need for exchange → Markets

Demand ↑ and Supply unchanged → Price ↑ and Quantity ↑
Supply ↑ and Demand unchanged → Price ↓ and Quantity ↑

Leakages > Injections → Economy contracts
Injections > Leakages → Economy expands

GDP growth ↑ → Unemployment ↓ → Inflation ↑ (Phillips Curve)

PPF shift OUT = Economic growth
PPF movement along = Opportunity cost (reallocation, not growth)
```

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing scarcity and shortage** — scarcity is permanent; shortage is temporary
2. **Saying opportunity cost is "what you give up"** — it's the BEST alternative given up
3. **Calling money a factor of production** — money is NOT a factor; it buys factors
4. **Confusing movement along a curve with a shift of the curve** — price changes move along; non-price factors shift the curve
5. **Saying the PPF shows all combinations** — it shows the MAXIMUM efficient combinations
6. **Confusing real and nominal GDP** — always use REAL GDP when comparing growth over time
7. **Treating all unemployment as one type** — distinguish cyclical, structural, frictional
8. **Stating a normative statement as if it's positive** — watch for "should," "ought," "better"
